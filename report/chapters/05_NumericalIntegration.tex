\section{Numerical Integration}

\subsection{Newton-Cotes Integration}

\begin{exercise}\label{ex:integrals}
	For each integral below, use the trapezoidal rule to estimate the integral
	for $m=10\cdot 2^k$ nodes for $k=1,2,\ldots,10$. Make a log-log plot of the
	errors and confirm or refute second-order accuracy.
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $\displaystyle \int_0^1 x\log(1+x)\, dx = \frac{1}{4}$

		\item $\displaystyle \int_0^1 x^2 \tan^{-1}x\, dx = \frac{\pi-2+2\log 2}{12}$

		\item $\displaystyle \int_0^{\pi/2}e^x \cos x\, dx = \frac{e^{\pi/2}-1}{2}$

		\item $\displaystyle \int_0^1 {\tan^{-1}(\sqrt{2+x^2})\over
				      (1+x^2)\sqrt{2+x^2}}dx={5\pi^2\over 96}$

		\item $\displaystyle \int_0^1 \sqrt{x} \log(x) \, dx = -\frac{4}{9}$
		      (Hint: Although the integrand has the limiting value zero as $x\to 0$,
		      it cannot be evaluated naively at $x=0$. One possibility is to start the
		      integral at $x=\epsilon_{\rm mach}$. Alternatively, you can define a
		      wrapper function $g(x)$ that returns $g(0)=0$ and $g(x)=\sqrt{x}\log(x)$
		      otherwise.)

		\item $\displaystyle \int_0^1 \sqrt{1-x^2}\,\, dx = \frac{\pi}{4}$
	\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
	To evaluate the given definite integrals, we employ the composite
	trapezoidal rule. For an interval $[a, b]$ discretized into $m$ equally
	spaced sub-intervals with step size $h = \frac{b-a}{m}$, the composite
	trapezoidal rule approximations are given by:
	\[
		\int_a^b f(x) \, dx \approx \frac{h}{2} \left( f(x_0) +
		2\sum_{i=1}^{m-1} f(x_i) + f(x_m) \right)
	\]
	where $x_i = a + i h$. The number of nodes was systematically increased
	according to $m = 10 \cdot 2^k$ for $k = 1, 2, \ldots, 10$, yielding a
	maximum of $10,240$ nodes.

	The error bound for the composite trapezoidal rule is theoretically given by:
	\[
		E \leq \frac{(b-a)}{12} h^2 \max_{x \in [a,b]} |f''(x)| = \mathcal{O}(h^2)
	\]
	This stipulates that the algorithm demonstrates second-order accuracy
	only if the integrand $f(x)$ is twice continuously differentiable,
	$f \in C^2[a,b]$.

	As observed in Table~\ref{tab:integral_results} and
	Figure~\ref{fig:abs_error}, all the integrals align with theoretical
	expectations, exhibiting strict second-order accuracy. In a log-log plot of
	error versus nodes $m$, this manifests as a line with a slope of $-2$.

\end{solution}

\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
	To evaluate the convergence improvements over the trapezoidal rule, we apply
	the composite Simpson's rule to the same set of integrals. For an interval
	$[a, b]$ discretized into $m$ equally spaced sub-intervals (where $m$ is even)
	with step size $h = \frac{b-a}{m}$, the approximation is given by:
	\[
		\int_a^b f(x) \, dx \approx \frac{h}{3} \left[ f(x_0) + 4
			\sum_{i=1,3,5\ldots}^{m-1} f(x_i) + 2 \sum_{i=2,4,6\ldots}^{m-2} f(x_i) +
			f(x_m) \right]
	\]
	Theoretical error analysis states that the absolute error for Simpson's rule
	is bounded by:
	\[
		E \leq \frac{(b-a)}{180} h^4 \max_{x \in [a,b]} |f^{(4)}(x)| =
		\mathcal{O}(h^4)
	\]
	This implies a theoretical fourth-order convergence ($\mathcal{O}(m^{-4})$),
	provided that the integrand is at least four times continuously
	differentiable, $f \in C^4[a,b]$.

	The integration results perfectly delineate the strict mathematical
	requirements of Simpson's rule:

	\begin{itemize}
		\item Smooth Integrands (Integrals 1--4): These functions satisfy
		      $f \in C^4[a,b]$. Because Simpson's rule scales at $\mathcal{O}(h^4)$, the
		      truncation error diminishes so rapidly that it essentially hits the
		      \texttt{Float64} machine epsilon limit at a fraction of the maximum
		      nodes. Further increases in node count merely accumulate numerical round-off
		      noise.
		\item Singular Integrands (Integrals 5--6): The fourth derivative
		      $f^{(4)}(x)$ approaches infinity at the boundaries for both
		      $\sqrt{x}\log(x)$ and $\sqrt{1-x^2}$. Consequently, the $\mathcal{O}(h^4)$
		      theoretical bound is entirely invalidated. Simpson's rule offers negligible
		      practical improvement over the trapezoidal rule for these functions,
		      remaining trapped at fractional convergence rates due to the dominant root
		      singularities.
	\end{itemize}

\end{solution}

\subsection{Free-nodes Integration}

\begin{exercise}
	Write a program that computes the nodes and weights for the Gauss-Legendre
	quadrature in the interval $[-1,1]$. Below are some tips for the
	implementation.

	\begin{itemize}
		\item To find the roots $x_k$, with $k=1,\ldots,n$ of the Legendre
		      polynomial $P_n(x)$ of order $n$ use Newton's method. Take as the initial
		      condition for the $k$th root,
		      \[
			      x^{(0)}_k=\cos(\phi_k)\,,
			      \qquad
			      \phi_k={4k-1\over 4n+2}\pi\,.
		      \]
		\item In order to use Newton's method you need to evaluate $P_n(x_k^{(i)})$
		      and the derivative $P'_n(x_k^{(i)})$, where $x_k^{(i)}$ is the $i$th
		      iteration in the search for the $k$th root $x_k$. You can obtain these by
		      first solving the recursion relation,

		      \[
			      (m+1)P_{m+1}(x) = (2m+1)xP_{m}(x) - mP_{m-1}(x)\,,
		      \]

		      for $m=1,\ldots,n-1$, where $P_0(x) = 1$ and $P_1(x) = x$, with
		      $x=x^{(i)}_k$. The derivative is then computed using the relation,

		      \[
			      (x^{2}-1)P'_{n}(x)=n[xP_{n}(x)-P_{n-1}(x)]\,.
		      \]

		\item Once you reach the root $x_k$ within some tolerance, the value of the
		      derivative $P'_n(x_k)$ allows you to obtain the corresponding weight using

		      \[
			      w_k= {2\over (1-x_k^2)[P'_n(x_k)]^2}\,.
		      \]
	\end{itemize}

\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}

	The Gauss-Legendre quadrature approximates the integral of a
	function over $[-1, 1]$ using a specific set of points (nodes) and
	corresponding weights. The nodes $x_k$ correspond to the roots of the $n$-th
	degree Legendre polynomial, $P_n(x) = 0$.

	The initial guesses $x_k^{(0)}$ are distributed according to the roots of the
	Chebyshev polynomials, which cluster near the boundaries $[-1, 1]$ and provide
	an excellent starting approximation for Legendre roots. The iteration
	terminates when successive approximations converge within machine precision
	($\approx 10^{-15}$).

\begin{exercise}

	For each improper integral, compute approximations using the proper
	double-exponential quadrature rule with $N=n/2$ and $n=4,6,8,\ldots,60$. Plot
	the errors as functions of $n$ on a semi-log scale.
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \dfrac{1}{1+x^2+x^4}\, dx = \dfrac{\pi}{\sqrt{3}}$
		\item $\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}\cos(x)\, dx = e^{-1/4}\sqrt{\pi}$
		\item $\int_{-\infty}^\infty (1+x^2)^{-2/3}\, dx =
			      \dfrac{\sqrt{\pi}\,\Gamma(1/6)}{\Gamma(2/3)}$
		\item $\int_{0}^{\infty} {1\over 1+x^2}\,dx = \frac{\pi}{2}$
		\item $\int_0^\infty \frac{e^{-x}}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi}$
	\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}

	Double-exponential (DE) quadrature is highly effective for evaluating improper
	integrals and integrals with endpoint singularities. The methodology relies on
	an analytic transformation $x = \phi(t)$ that maps the integration domain into
	$(-\infty, \infty)$, such that the integrand decays double-exponentially at the
	boundaries:
	\[
		f(\phi(t)) \phi'(t) \sim \exp(-C \exp(|t|)) \quad \text{as } t \to \pm \infty
	\]
	This exceptionally rapid decay allows the integral to be truncated to a finite
	interval and evaluated using the trapezoidal rule, yielding spectral-like
	convergence.

	For infinite domains $(-\infty, \infty)$ (Integrals 1--3), we apply the
	standard mapping:
	\[
		x(t) = \sinh\left(\frac{\pi}{2} \sinh(t)\right)
	\]
	For semi-infinite domains $(0, \infty)$ (Integral 4), we apply:
	\[
		x(t) = \exp\left(\frac{\pi}{2} \sinh(t)\right)
	\]
	Integral 5 features an algebraic singularity at the origin $\left(x^{-1/2} \to
		\infty \text{ as } x \to 0\right)$. To optimize numerical stability at this
	boundary, an alternative mapping is employed:
	\[
		x(t) = \exp(t - \exp(-t))
	\]

	As theoretically expected, the convergence rates displayed in the accompanying
	figures are steep. For all five integrands, including Integral 5, which

	approaches the double-precision noise floor $\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}$ by $n

	yields no further analytical benefit; rather, it introduces catastrophic
	cancellation due to the accumulation of floating-point rounding errors.

	Furthermore, employing the alternative substitution $x(t) = \exp(t -
		\exp(-t))$ for Integral 5 successfully mitigated numerical instability near the
	singular origin $x=0$, enabling a convergence profile nearly identical to the
	strictly smooth analytic integrals.

\begin{exercise}
	For each integral, compute approximations using the Gauss-Legendre quadrature
	rule with $n=4,6,8,\ldots,60$, and the proper double-exponential quadrature rule
	with $N=n/2$. Plot the errors as functions of $n$ on a semi-log scale.
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $\int_{0}^1 \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\, dx = \dfrac{\pi}{2}$
		\item $\int_0^1 \sqrt{x} \log(x) \, dx = -\frac{4}{9}$
		\item $\int_0^1 \sqrt{1-x^2}\,\, dx = \frac{\pi}{4}$
		\item $\int_{0}^1 (\log x)^2\, dx = 2$
		\item $\int_{0}^{\pi/2} \log(\cos(x))\, dx = -\frac{\pi}{2}\log(2)$
		\item $\int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sqrt{\tan(x)}\, dx = \dfrac{\pi}{\sqrt{2}}$
	\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}

	Standard Gaussian quadrature routines are derived under the assumption that
	the integrand is infinitely continuously differentiable ($C^\infty$) over the
	integration domain. When functions exhibit algebraic or logarithmic
	singularities at the boundary (e.g., $1/\sqrt{1-x^2}$ as $x \to 1$, or $(\log
		x)^2$ as $x \to 0$), the global smoothness assumption breaks down, severely
	degrading the GLQ convergence rate from exponential to merely algebraic.

	Conversely, Double-Exponential Quadrature restores spectral-like convergence
	by employing a nonlinear conformal mapping that flattens these boundary
	singularities. For a finite integration interval $(a, b)$, the standard
	tanh-sinh mapping is applied:
	\[
		x(t) = \frac{b-a}{2} \tanh\left(\frac{\pi}{2} \sinh(t)\right) + \frac{b+a}{2}
	\]
	This translates the physical domain $x \in (a, b)$ into an infinite
	computational domain $t \in (-\infty, \infty)$. The Jacobian of this
	transformation decays double-exponentially as $|t| \to \infty$, successfully
	overwhelming boundary singularities and permitting highly efficient truncation
	of the computational domain. To strictly ensure fairness between the two
	methodologies, the DE rule utilizes $N = n/2$ nodes, guaranteeing the total
	number of function evaluations remains theoretically balanced for direct
	comparison with GLQ.

	The numerical experiments clearly demonstrate the superior performance of

	non-smooth behavior. While Gauss--Legendre quadrature remains highly effective
	for analytic integrands, its convergence deteriorates substantially when the
	smoothness assumptions are violated. The tanh-sinh transformation employed in
	DEQ successfully regularizes these singular behaviors, enabling rapid
	convergence to near machine precision with comparatively few function
	evaluations. The observed error trends and benchmark comparisons therefore
	validate the theoretical advantages of DEQ as a robust and highly accurate
	method for evaluating improper and singular integrals.

\subsection{Some observations about the algorithms used in this section}

The two empty entries in the accuracy column for the trapezoidal and Simpson
rules are intentional: in this test they are used to estimate the empirical
order of convergence, not to certify a single final error. Conversely, the
free-node and transformed rules are tested on benchmark integrals for which the
expected value is known exactly or to high precision, so their reported metric is
the final absolute error. The Gauss--Legendre check integrates $x^5$ over
$[-1,1]$ using a three-point rule, where exactness up to degree $2n-1=5$ gives a
zero error in floating-point arithmetic. Fejér and Clenshaw--Curtis both recover
the quadratic test integral to round-off accuracy, and the double-exponential
rule evaluates the Gaussian integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}e^{-x^2}\,dx$ with
an error of order $10^{-15}$.

The first two rows confirm the classical orders of the composite Newton--Cotes
rules. The composite trapezoidal rule is a closed equally spaced rule based on a
linear interpolant, and is exact for polynomials of degree at most one. Its
global truncation error is $\mathcal{O}(h^2)$ when the integrand is sufficiently
smooth, so halving the step size reduces the leading error by approximately a
factor of four. Simpson's rule is also closed and equally spaced, but uses the
endpoints and midpoint of each panel. Although it is derived from a quadratic
interpolant, its symmetry makes it exact for cubic polynomials, and the smooth
case improves to $\mathcal{O}(h^4)$. The measured rates, $2.000186$ and
$4.000332$, therefore match the theoretical second- and fourth-order behavior.

Gauss--Legendre quadrature belongs to the class of free-node rules. Its nodes are
chosen as the zeros of Legendre polynomials rather than being fixed on an
equally spaced grid. This choice maximizes the algebraic degree of exactness:
with $n$ nodes, the rule is exact for every polynomial of degree at most
$2n-1$. For analytic integrands this usually produces geometric convergence, and
the positivity of the weights provides good numerical stability. The principal
practical drawback is that the nodes are not nested: increasing $n$ changes the
interior nodes, so function values from a lower-order computation are generally
not reusable.

Fejér and Clenshaw--Curtis quadrature use Chebyshev points and are therefore
closely related to polynomial interpolation in a Chebyshev basis. The Fejér rule
implemented here uses the zeros of the Chebyshev polynomial and avoids the
endpoints, while Clenshaw--Curtis uses Chebyshev extrema and therefore includes
the interval endpoints. These rules typically do not maximize the degree of
exactness in the same way as Gaussian quadrature, but they are simple, stable,
and computationally attractive. Their Chebyshev structure also makes nested or
partly reusable refinements natural in many implementations.

The double-exponential rule, also known as tanh--sinh quadrature on finite
intervals, changes variables before applying a trapezoidal rule. For
$[-1,1]$ the characteristic map is
\[
	x=\tanh\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\sinh(t)\right),
\]
whose derivative decays extremely rapidly as $|t|$ grows. This transformation is
especially effective for endpoint singularities: the Jacobian suppresses the
singular behavior at the physical interval boundaries and leaves a transformed
integrand that is well suited to the trapezoidal rule on a truncated infinite
interval.
