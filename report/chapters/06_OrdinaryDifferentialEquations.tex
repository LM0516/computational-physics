\begin{solution}

	The explicit Euler method is a first-order Runge-Kutta numerical integration
	scheme for solving initial value problems (IVPs) of the form $u' = f(t, u)$.
	Given a temporal domain $[t_0, t_f]$ discretized into $n$ steps of uniform size
	$h = (t_f - t_0)/n$, the system is iteratively updated via:
	\[
		u_{i+1} = u_i + h f(t_i, u_i)
	\]
	Because the method relies on a truncated Taylor series approximation, the
	local truncation error introduced at each discrete step scales as
	$\mathcal{O}(h^2)$. However, as these errors accumulate sequentially over $n
		\propto h^{-1}$ steps, the global error bounds scale as $\mathcal{O}(h)$,
	mathematically equivalent to $\mathcal{O}(n^{-1})$.

	To empirically verify this theoretical first-order convergence, we compute
	explicit Euler solutions across progressively refined grids where $n = 10 \cdot
		2^k$ for $k \in \{2, 3, \dots, 10\}$. Two distinct error metrics are recorded:
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item The global maximum absolute divergence ($L^\infty$ norm):
		      $||u-\hat{u}||_\infty = \max_{i} |u_i-\hat{u}(t_i)|$.
		\item The final accumulated displacement at the domain boundary:
		      $E_{\text{final}} = |u_n-\hat{u}(t_n)|$.
	\end{enumerate}

	\paragraph{Results \& Discussion}
	For the baseline resolution of $n=320$, the numerical solutions smoothly
	trace the analytical trajectories for all three IVPs. Because $h$ is
	sufficiently small, virtually zero visually distinguishable deviation is
	observed on linear scales.

	Both the maximum absolute error arrays ($L^\infty$) and the final boundary
	error arrays run parallel to the theoretical $\mathcal{O}(n^{-1})$ reference
	lines. By definition, the final-time error is bounded from above by the
	$L^\infty$ norm ($|u_n - \hat{u}(t_n)| \leq ||u-\hat{u}||_\infty$).

	The transient numerical solutions at $n=320$ (left column) and the

\begin{solution}

	To apply the first-order forward Euler method, each second-order ordinary
	differential equation (ODE) must be reduced to a system of coupled first-order
	ODEs. We introduce the state vector $\mathbf{u}(t) = [u_1(t), u_2(t)]^\intercal
			= [y(t), y'(t)]^\intercal$. The general second-order ODE $y'' = f(t, y, y')$ can
	thus be expressed as:
	\[
		\mathbf{u}'(t) =
		\begin{bmatrix}
			u_1'(t) \\
			u_2'(t)
		\end{bmatrix}
		=
		\begin{bmatrix}
			u_2(t) \\
			f(t, u_1(t), u_2(t))
		\end{bmatrix}
	\]
	The explicit Euler update scheme discretizes time into steps $t_k = a + kh$,
	where $h = (b-a)/n$ is the uniform step size. The numerical approximation
	$\mathbf{u}_k \approx \mathbf{u}(t_k)$ advances via:
	\[
		\mathbf{u}_{k+1} = \mathbf{u}_k + h \mathbf{f}(t_k, \mathbf{u}_k)
	\]
	For the given problems, the specific governing systems become:
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $\mathbf{u}' = [u_2, \: \sin(2t) - 9u_1]^\intercal$
		\item $\mathbf{u}' = [u_2, \: 4t + 4u_1]^\intercal$
		\item $\mathbf{u}' = [u_2, \: t - 4u_2 - 4u_1]^\intercal$
	\end{enumerate}

	\paragraph{Results}
	The integration was performed with $n = 1000$ steps for all three systems.
	The step sizes correspond to $h_1 \approx 0.00628$, $h_2 = 0.0015$, and $h_3 =

	\paragraph{Discussion}
	The numerical results reflect the global truncation error characteristic of
	the first-order forward Euler method, $\mathcal{O}(h)$. Although $n=1000$
	guarantees a relatively small step size $h$, the stability of the method plays a
	critical role in the error propagation:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item System 1 (Oscillatory): The homogeneous part of the ODE
		      yields purely imaginary eigenvalues ($\lambda = \pm 3i$). The stability region
		      of the explicit Euler method strictly excludes the imaginary axis. Consequently,
		      the numerical solution unconditionally amplifies the oscillations over time,
		      leading to a linearly bounding envelope of the absolute error as a phase drift
		      occurs.
		\item System 2 (Exponential Growth): The exact solution
		      exhibits an exponentially growing term $e^{2t}$. Since the function values grow
		      rapidly, the local truncation error, which is proportional to $y''(t)$, also
		      scales exponentially. Thus, the absolute error diverges dramatically as $t \to
			      1.5$.
		\item System 3 (Exponential Decay): Due to the dampening factor
		      $e^{-2t}$, the exact transient solution decays toward the linear particular
		      solution. The absolute error peaks during the transient phase near $t=0.5$ and
		      subsequently decays. This behavior demonstrates that Euler's method performs
		      relatively well when the underlying system's dynamics are contractive (stable
		      eigenvalues).
	\end{itemize}

	\paragraph{Conclusion}
	The first-order Euler method captures the basic phenomenological behavior of
	the three systems for the chosen step sizes.

\end{solution}

\begin{solution}

	To numerically integrate the given initial value problem (IVP),
	\[
		u' = -2t u, \quad u(0) = 2, \quad t \in [0, 2],
	\]
	we employ two explicit Runge-Kutta (RK) methods: the second-order Improved
	Euler (IE2) method and the classical fourth-order Runge-Kutta (RK4) method. The
	exact analytical solution is given by $\hat{u}(t) = 2e^{-t^2}$.

	To assess the performance of these numerical schemes, it is insufficient to
	solely compare the error against the step size $h = \frac{b-a}{n}$. Instead, we
	must account for the computational cost of each algorithm. The IE2 scheme
	requires two function evaluations per time step, whereas the RK4 scheme
	necessitates four. Therefore, for $n$ integration steps, the total number of
	function evaluations is $N_{\text{eval}}^{\text{IE2}} = 2n$ and
	$N_{\text{eval}}^{\text{RK4}} = 4n$, respectively.

	The global truncation error is quantified using the Chebyshev ($L^\infty$)
	norm of the absolute error across all discrete time steps:
	\[
		E_\infty(n) = \max_{0\leq i\leq n} |u_i - \hat{u}(t_i)|.
	\]
	Given the theoretical convergence orders, we expect the global error to scale
	as $E \propto h^p$. Expressing $h$ in terms of the total function evaluations
	$N_{\text{eval}}$, we expect the asymptotic behavior to strictly follow $E
		\propto (N_{\text{eval}})^{-p}$, where $p=2$ for IE2 and $p=4$ for RK4.

	\paragraph{Results}
	The resulting log-log plot (Figure~\ref{fig:ie2_rk4_convergence}) illustrates
	the maximum absolute error as a function of the computational workload (function
	evaluations). The dashed and dotted black lines indicate the theoretically
	predicted algebraic decay rates.

	\begin{center}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/6-3/IE2-vs-RK4.pdf}
		\captionof{figure}{Global truncation error of the IE2 and RK4 algorithms as
			a function of total function evaluations. Both schemes perfectly parallel their
			theoretical reference slopes of $-2$ and $-4$, respectively.}
		\label{fig:ie2_rk4_convergence}
	\end{center}

	\paragraph{Discussion}
	The empirically observed convergence rates are in agreement with the
	theoretical expectations. Specifically, on the log-log scale, the IE2 error
	curve exhibits a slope of $-2$, confirming its second-order nature, while the
	RK4 curve exhibits a steeper slope of $-4$.

	Crucially, standardizing the horizontal axis to measure \emph{function
		evaluations} provides a fair comparison of algorithmic efficiency. Even though
	RK4 requires twice as much computational work per step compared to IE2, its
	superior convergence order rapidly outpaces the computational overhead.

	\paragraph{Conclusion}
	We have successfully validated the implementation of the IE2 and RK4
	integrators. The work-precision analysis confirms that higher-order schemes like
	RK4 are computationally optimal for smooth functions requiring stringent
	accuracy tolerances, provided the step sizes are kept well above the threshold
	where machine precision artifacts arise.

\end{solution}

	\paragraph{Discussion}
	The observed numerical results can be validated by transforming the systems into polar coordinates ($x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$).
	For System (a), the radial dynamics simplify to:
	\[
		r' = r(1-r^2).
	\]
	Here, $r=1$ is a stable fixed point of the radial ODE, which manifests as a globally stable limit cycle in the Cartesian plane. Any perturbation away from $r=1$ is naturally suppressed by the dynamical system.

	Conversely, for System (b), the radial ODE is given by:
	\[
		r' = -\frac{1}{4} r (1-r^2)(4-r^2).
	\]
	The roots of this polynomial dictate the invariant sets: $r=0$, $r=1$, and $r=2$. Initial conditions with $r < 1$ (e.g., $[0.95, 0]$) are attracted to the stable origin $r=0$. Initial conditions with $r > 1$ (e.g., $[0, 1.05]$ and $[-2.5, 0]$) are pushed away from $r=1$ and pulled toward the stable limit cycle at $r=2$.

	\paragraph{Conclusion}
	The RK4 integrator flawlessly captured the limiting behaviors, including both stable and unstable limit cycles.

The system is described by the time-dependent Schr\"odinger equation (TDSE) governed by the potential $V(x) = 2$ for $x \in [-1, 1]$, and $V(x) = 0$ elsewhere. The initial state is a moving Gaussian wave packet centered at $x_0 = -20.0$, with a spatial dispersion $\sigma = 2.0$ and a momentum of $k_0 = 2.0$:

\begin{solution}

	We aim to solve the time-independent Schr\"odinger equation (TISE), $\hat{H}\varphi_n = E_n\varphi_n$, for a finite square well potential. Assuming natural units where $\hbar^2/2m = 1$, the continuous kinetic energy operator is approximated using a second-order central finite difference scheme. The spatial domain is truncated to $x \in [-5, 5]$ and discretized into a uniform grid with spacing $\Delta x = 0.05$. 

	This discretization maps the continuous Hamiltonian operator $\hat{H} = -\frac{d^2}{dx^2} + V(x)$ into a finite-dimensional, symmetric, tridiagonal matrix $H$. The spectral decomposition of $H$ is performed using the pure QR algorithm limited to 200 iterations. The bound states correspond to the eigenvectors whose associated eigenvalues (energies) satisfy $E_n < 0$.

	Regarding the normalization of the wave functions, numerical integration of the squared wave function is unnecessary. Because $H$ is a real symmetric matrix, its eigenvectors $v^{(n)}$ are mutually orthogonal and intrinsically normalized with respect to the discrete Euclidean norm, $\sum_i |v^{(n)}_i|^2 = 1$. To map this discrete vector to the continuous $L_2$-normalized wave function $\varphi_n(x_i)$, we simply scale the eigenvector by the square root of the grid spacing:
	\[
			\int |\varphi_n(x)|^2 \, dx \approx \sum_i |\varphi_n(x_i)|^2 \Delta x = 1 \implies \varphi_n(x_i) = \frac{v^{(n)}_i}{\sqrt{\Delta x}}.
	\]

	\paragraph{Results}
	The spectral analysis yields exactly three bound states ($E_n < 0$) localized within the potential well. The computed energy eigenvalues are:
	\begin{align*}
			E_1 &= -9.1473 \quad \text{(Ground state, even parity)}\\
			E_2 &= -6.6501 \quad \text{(First excited state, odd parity)}\\
			E_3 &= -2.8071 \quad \text{(Second excited state, even parity)}
	\end{align*}
	It should be noted that while the pure QR algorithm triggered a convergence warning at 200 iterations, the bound state eigenvalues at the lower extremum of the spectrum effectively converge much faster than the dense positive energy continuum.

	The uncoupled, normalized probability densities $|\varphi_n(x)|^2$ corresponding to these bound states are superimposed onto their respective energy levels $E_n$ and plotted alongside the potential well in Figure \ref{fig:bound_states}.

			\label{fig:bound_states}
	\end{center}

	\paragraph{Discussion}
	The numerical solutions exhibit excellent qualitative agreement with quantum mechanical principles. The wave functions strictly alternate in parity, with the ground state possessing zero nodes, the first excited state possessing one node, and the second excited state possessing two nodes. As energy increases ($E_1 \rightarrow E_3$), the wave function tails extend further into the classically forbidden region ($|x| > 1$), illustrating the increased probability of quantum tunneling for less tightly bound states.

	The finite difference discretization combined with the QR algorithm successfully identifies the complete set of bound states for the finite square well. \subsection{Some observations about the algorithms used in this section}

The numerical results reported in this section are validated by the Julia test
suites in \texttt{ode\_tests.jl} and
\texttt{schrodinger\_tests.jl}. The first file checks the
empirical convergence rates and function-evaluation counts for the ODE
integrators, while the second verifies the main structural properties of the
Schr\"odinger solvers, including sparse Hermitian Hamiltonian construction and
approximate conservation of the wave-function norm.

\paragraph{Measured ODE performance}
For the scalar test problem $u'=u$ on $[0,1]$, the ODE tests estimate the
observed convergence orders by refining the mesh and comparing the final-time
error with the exact value $e$. They also count the number of calls to the
right-hand side for $n=25$ steps. The resulting work-precision summary is:

\begin{center}
	\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
	\begin{tabular}{lcc}
		\toprule
		Method & Convergence rate & Computational cost \\
		\midrule
		Euler & $0.963913$ & $25$ evaluations ($n=25$) \\
		IE2   & $1.968661$ & $50$ evaluations ($n=25$) \\
		RK4   & $3.965480$ & $100$ evaluations ($n=25$) \\
		\bottomrule
	\end{tabular}
\end{center}

These rates closely match the theoretical orders $p=1$, $p=2$, and $p=4$,
respectively. The slight deviations from integer values are expected because the
orders are estimated from finite step sizes rather than from the asymptotic limit
$h\to0$.

\paragraph{Euler method}
The explicit Euler method is the simplest one-step scheme. Starting from
$u_i\approx u(t_i)$, it advances the solution by following the tangent line at
the current point,
\[
	u_{i+1}=u_i+h f(t_i,u_i).
\]
Its main advantage is cost: each step requires only one evaluation of
$f(t,u)$. This makes it useful for introducing time discretization and for cheap
qualitative experiments.

The price of this simplicity is low accuracy and restrictive stability. The
local truncation error is $\mathcal{O}(h^2)$, but the accumulated global error is
only $\mathcal{O}(h)$. For the scalar stability test equation $u'=\lambda u$
with $\lambda<0$, the amplification factor is $1+h\lambda$, so stability on the
negative real axis requires
\[
	|1+h\lambda|<1,
	\qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad
	h<\frac{2}{|\lambda|}.
\]
Thus Euler's method can oscillate or diverge unless the time step is chosen very
small. This limitation is especially visible for oscillatory systems, where the
explicit Euler stability region does not include the imaginary axis.

\paragraph{Improved Euler method}
The Improved Euler method used here is the midpoint second-order Runge-Kutta
scheme. It first evaluates the slope at the beginning of the interval, uses that
slope to predict the midpoint value, and then takes the full step using the
midpoint slope:
\[
	u_{i+1}
	= u_i + h f\left(t_i+\frac{h}{2},
	u_i+\frac{h}{2}f(t_i,u_i)\right).
\]
Because it samples the vector field inside the interval rather than only at its
left endpoint, it cancels the leading global error term of Euler's method. Its
local truncation error is $\mathcal{O}(h^3)$ and its global error is
$\mathcal{O}(h^2)$.

The method requires two evaluations of $f$ per step, so for the same number of
steps it costs twice as much as Euler. In return, the error decreases
quadratically with the step size, which usually makes IE2 much more efficient
than Euler for smooth problems. It belongs to the broader family of second-order
Runge-Kutta methods, alongside variants such as Heun's method and the modified
Euler method.

\paragraph{Fourth-order Runge-Kutta method}
The classical fourth-order Runge-Kutta method (RK4) is a four-stage scheme. It
computes four slopes,
\[
\begin{aligned}
	k_1 &= f(t_i,u_i),\\
	k_2 &= f\left(t_i+\frac{h}{2},u_i+\frac{h}{2}k_1\right),\\
	k_3 &= f\left(t_i+\frac{h}{2},u_i+\frac{h}{2}k_2\right),\\
	k_4 &= f(t_i+h,u_i+hk_3),
\end{aligned}
\]
and combines them as
\[
	u_{i+1}=u_i+\frac{h}{6}(k_1+2k_2+2k_3+k_4).
\]
The resulting global error is $\mathcal{O}(h^4)$, so reducing the step size gives
a much faster decrease in error than for Euler or IE2.

RK4 requires four evaluations of the right-hand side per step. Nevertheless, the
large gain in accuracy often outweighs this cost, which is why RK4 is a standard
choice in many computational physics calculations when the solution is smooth
and fixed step sizes are adequate.

\paragraph{Summary comparison}
Overall, Euler's method is most useful for conceptual understanding and simple
diagnostics. IE2 is a meaningful improvement at modest extra cost. RK4 is the
preferred practical method for many of the ordinary differential equation
examples in this section because its fourth-order convergence gives high
accuracy before the cost of function evaluations becomes dominant.
